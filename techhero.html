Mark Dean

“A lot of kids growing up today aren’t told that you can be whatever you want to be. There may be obstacles, but there are no limits.” -Mark Dean

About His Work

Dr. Mark Dean is a prominent figure in the world of computers, he is an inventor and computer engineer. He is most known for his contributions at IBM on the invention of the Personal Computer (PC), he holds three out of nine IBM patents on the PC. Him and his co-creator, Dennis Moeller, paved the way in Information Technology by allowing the use of plug-in subsystems and peripherals like disk drives, video gear, speakers, and scanners. In addition to his work on the PC, he also lead a design team that produced the 1-Gigahertz chip, an invention with limitless potential. Eventually, Dr. Mark Dean joined the faculty of the College of Engineering at his alma mater, the Univeristy of Tennessee. He is now retired and currently holds over 40 patents for his work.

His time working in the computer industry helped set precedent for African American people following in his footsteps. In 1995, Mark became the first African American to be named an IBM Fellow.

Honors

  • 1997: Received Black Engineer of the Year Presdient's Award
  • 1997: Inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame

Anna Easley

"I just have my own attitude. I’m out here to get the job done, and I knew I had the ability to do it, and that’s where my focus was." -Annie Easley

About Her Work

Annie Easley was a pioneering African American computer scientist, mathematician, and rocket scientist who made significant contributions to NASA's space programs. She worked at the Lewis Research Center (now NASA Glenn Research Center), where she developed software for energy conversion systems and rocket propulsion technologies. Easley was instrumental in advancing the Centaur rocket project, a high-energy rocket essential for launching space probes. In addition to her technical work, she advocated for diversity and inclusion in STEM fields, inspiring future generations of women and minorities to pursue careers in science and engineering.

Easley began her career as a “human computer,” doing computations for researchers. This involved analyzing problems and doing calculations by hand. Her earliest work involved running simulations for the newly planned Plum Brook Reactor Facility. When hired, she was one of only four African-American employees at the Lab. Easley retired in 1989, but she remained an active participant in the Speaker’s Bureau and the Business & Professional Women’s Association. Annie Easley passed away on June 25, 2011.

Honors

1970: Annie Easley received a Special Achievement Award from Director of Administration Henry Barnett and Deputy Director Gene Manganiello

2001: She was awarded the NASA Exceptional Performance Award for her work on energy conversion systems.

Lucy Koh

"I will not let any theatrics or any sideshow distract us from what we are here to do" -Lucy Koh

About Their Work

Judge Lucy Koh became known as the Referee of Silicon Valley during her time as a United States District Judge for the Northern District of California from 2010 - 2021. She has presided over cases involving large tech companies such as Samsung and Apple, Google, Youtube, Twitter, Verizon, and more. She has worked on issues ranging from patent rights, privacy in the digital age, and worker’s rights. It is through these judicial rulings that she has made significant contributions to the tech world. Some notable cases include the patent litigation between Apple and Samsung, as well as class-action lawsuits related to data breaches at Yahoo and Anthem

In addition to her work on data privacy and intellectual property, Judge Koh ruled in favor of tech workers in a landmark case against several tech giants accused of colluding to suppress wages by agreeing not to hire each other’s employees3. Her decisions have had a lasting impact on the tech industry, promoting fair competition and protecting workers’ rights
